  • TYPE: Mammals
  • DIET: Herbivore

Issues Facing African Elephants

  • Habitat destruction by humans
  • Poaching for ivory
  • Human-elephant conflict due to competition for the same land and resources
  • Disease
  • Their abiility to travel large distances makes them hard to keep track of

How can Computer Science help?

Over the last century the population of African Elephants (Loxodonta africana) has plummeted to 415,000 and they are listed as "vulnerable" on the IUCN Red List of Threatened Species. This is due to poaching, retaliatory killing for crop raiding and habitat fragmentation. Developments in Computer Science have meant that we can help these majestic animals to survive poachers and even allow us to count them from space.

Using drones to prevent poaching.

Right now thousands of drones are flying over wildlife parks in South Africa, equipped with thermal infrared cameras and smart automatic detection systems that can identify potential poachers and alert ranger. These are effective but often rangers are not nearby since the parks are so huge. This has led to a new paper being published by Computer Scientists at Harvard John A. Paulson School of Engineering and Applied Sciences that looks at how we can prevent poaching without the need for rangers.

"We’ve turned our uncertainties into our advantage."

Elizabeth Bondi, SEAS graduite student

The algorithm, called GUARDSS uses the uncertainty in the drones the readings to dissuade poachers; if a drone sees a poacher it will signal if there is a ranger nearby, if not then it will calculate randomly whether it should signal or not. It also signals randomly when it sees nothing.This means that the poachers don't know if they've been seen or not so they can't know to run away giving the rangers a significant advantage in the fight against ivory poaching.

Counting Elephants from space.

An algorithm developed by Dr Olgoa Isupova , a computer scientist at the University of Bath, is using satellite imagery from worldview 3 and 4 alongside deeplearning to count elephants in the South African Savanna. The deeplearniing was done using Google's Tensor Flow API which fed a customised training data set of over 1000 elephants into a Convolutional Neural Network(CNN)

Similar projects have been undergone before. For example NASA used satellite imagery to find a secret penguin colony. However it has never been done before in a heterogenous landscape like the African Savanna.

The result of this training, (known as the F2 Score ) for the CNN models, 0.78 in heterogeneous areas and 0.73 in homogenous areas, compared with, an averaged human detection capability F2 score of 0.77 in heterogeneous areas and 0.80 in homogenous areas. This technology is paramount for the conservation of African Elephants since it allows for 5,000 km² of imagery to be collected and analysed in a matter of hours where before it would have taken a group of people weeks in aircraft to accomplish the same feat. This vital data about the elephants numbers and weherabouts is imperative to rangers and conservationists so the teams at Bath and Oxford are working on moving towards smaller animals.
